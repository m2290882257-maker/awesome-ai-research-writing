# Personal Academic Writing Rules

## Scope

This rule set is for bilingual academic writing in Chinese and English across film studies, digital media art, new media art theory, design studies, interaction design, experience design, HCI, human-AI co-creation, media and communication studies, and visual culture studies.

Target paper types include theoretical papers, artwork analysis, design research, HCI experiments, and artistic practice research.

Target venues and indexes include Beijing Film Academy Journal, Contemporary Cinema, Film Art, World Cinema, Movie Review, Movie Literature, Hundred Schools in Arts, Chinese Literature and Art Criticism, Zhuangshi, Art and Design Research, Journal of Nanjing Arts Institute, Journal of Design, Packaging Engineering, SIGGRAPH, SIGGRAPH Asia Art Papers, ISEA, EVA London, ACM MM, SIGGRAPH Technical Papers, CVPR Workshops, CSSCI, CSSCI Extended, Peking University Core Journals, AMI, Chinese Science and Technology Core Journals, CSCD, high-quality scientific journal rankings, and CCF recommended conferences and journals.

## Core Principles

### Restrained Revision

Revise only to improve clarity, conceptual accuracy, argumentative coherence, and venue fit. If the original text is already accurate, natural, and academically appropriate, preserve it rather than rewriting for cosmetic sophistication.

### No Fabrication

Do not invent theories, references, author claims, artwork details, artist intentions, technical parameters, experimental data, interview content, user feedback, exhibition information, or publication requirements. Every claim must be grounded in supplied text, verifiable sources, close reading, field notes, user research, experimental data, interviews, or exhibition observation.

### Clean LaTeX

When working with LaTeX, keep the source clean and maintainable. Do not add unnecessary bold, italics, decorative formatting, or complex commands. Emphasis should come from argument structure and wording rather than visual formatting.

### Evidence and Data Driven

Claims about experiments, user studies, interaction experience, media analysis, communication effects, visual culture samples, or case comparisons must be supported by materials, data, textual evidence, or observation records. Qualitative research should also state sample sources, analytical dimensions, and grounds for interpretation.

### Reviewer Perspective

Always inspect the text as a reviewer would: Is the research question clear? Is the theoretical framework justified? Is the literature positioning accurate? Do cases, experiments, or materials support the claims? Is the contribution real? Are methods reliable? Are concepts mixed? Does the conclusion exceed the evidence?

## 1. Chinese Polishing

Use for Chinese paper sections, abstracts, literature reviews, artwork analysis, theoretical argumentation, and pre-submission language polishing.

Rules:
- Use restrained revision and preserve the author's meaning.
- Remove colloquial wording, promotional tone, translationese, and empty adjectives.
- Use Word-friendly plain text without Markdown markers.
- Keep terminology stable, especially terms such as immersion, interaction, mediality, embodiment, and human-AI co-creation.
- Prefer steady, clear, and restrained academic prose.

Check:
- Is there colloquial expression?
- Is there conceptual name-dropping without argument?
- Is there unsupported subjective exaggeration?
- Are Chinese and English terms used consistently?
- Does the tone fit the target Chinese journal?

Output:
- Part 1 [Refined Text]
- Part 2 [Revision Notes]

## 2. English Polishing

Use for English paper text, abstracts, introductions, related work, methods, discussions, and LaTeX fragments.

Rules:
- Keep LaTeX clean.
- Do not add unnecessary bold, italics, or complex formatting.
- Use clear, direct English that cross-disciplinary reviewers can understand.
- Avoid AI-like wording, promotional adjectives, and unnecessarily complex vocabulary.
- Preserve technical terms, citation commands, equations, and LaTeX structure.

Check:
- Are grammar, articles, tense, and prepositions correct?
- Do long sentences reduce clarity?
- Are key terms consistent?
- Is there any overclaim?
- Are humanities, design, or HCI concepts misused?
- Does the tone fit ACM, SIGGRAPH, ISEA, EVA London, or the target venue?

Output:
- Part 1 [Polished LaTeX / English Text]
- Part 2 [Chinese Literal Translation]
- Part 3 [Modification Log]

## 3. Theoretical Paper Rewriting

Use for theoretical papers in film theory, media theory, visual culture, phenomenology, posthumanism, STS, and design research.

Rules:
- Do not invent theoretical sources or literature claims.
- Theory must serve the research question rather than decorate the text.
- Define key concepts when they first appear in the paper's context.
- Avoid stacking theoretical terms without argument.
- Logic may be reorganized, but new theoretical claims must not be added without evidence.

Check:
- Is the research question clear?
- Does the theoretical framework fit the object of analysis?
- Are core concepts defined?
- Does the literature review identify the research gap?
- Does the argument move from concepts to materials rather than staying abstract?
- Does the conclusion exceed the argument?

Recommended structure:
Research question -> Conceptual definition -> Literature context -> Theoretical framework -> Analytical path -> Contribution.

Output:
- Part 1 [Rewritten Text]
- Part 2 [Argument Structure]
- Part 3 [Items to Verify or Supplement]

## 4. Artwork Analysis Expansion

Use for film, moving image art, digital media art, new media installation, interactive artwork, exhibition projects, and AI-generated art analysis.

Rules:
- Expansion must be based on artwork details.
- Do not invent artwork content, artist intention, exhibition information, or audience response.
- Move from concrete media features to theoretical analysis.
- Avoid only summarizing themes. Analyze form, structure, medium, and viewing or interaction experience.

Check:
- Does the analysis mention concrete details such as shots, sound, editing, narrative, interface, space, interaction path, generative mechanism, or exhibition environment?
- Does it explain how viewers or users participate?
- Does it distinguish description, analytical judgment, and theoretical interpretation?
- Is there enough evidence for terms such as immersion, interactivity, and co-creation?

Recommended structure:
Artwork description -> Formal / technical / interaction mechanism -> Viewing or participation experience -> Theoretical interpretation -> Research significance.

Output:
- Part 1 [Expanded Text]
- Part 2 [Basis for Added Content]
- Part 3 [Unverified Information]

## 5. HCI Experiment Paper Review

Use for interaction design, experience design, HCI, user studies, experimental evaluation, system papers, and AI co-creation tool evaluation.

Rules:
- Be evidence and data driven.
- Do not generalize individual observations into universal conclusions.
- Make experimental design, samples, tasks, measures, and analysis transparent.
- Distinguish design claims, user feedback, experimental results, and author interpretation.
- Match conclusions to the strength of the data.

Check:
- Is the research question testable?
- Is participant information sufficient?
- Are experimental tasks reasonable?
- Do measures correspond to the research question?
- Are statistical methods appropriate?
- Is qualitative coding explained?
- Is user feedback overinterpreted?
- Are limitations honestly presented?

Recommended structure:
Research Question -> Participants -> Apparatus / Prototype -> Tasks -> Measures -> Procedure -> Results -> Discussion -> Limitations.

Output:
- Part 1 [Review Report]
- Part 2 [Key Risks]
- Part 3 [Revision Suggestions]
- Part 4 [Missing Data or Clarifications]

## 6. Pre-submission Reviewer Check

Use before submission to Chinese core journals, CSSCI journals, art and design journals, ACM, SIGGRAPH, ISEA, EVA London, or related venues.

Rules:
- Use a reviewer perspective.
- Distinguish critical problems from fixable problems.
- Do not focus on minor stylistic preferences.
- Directly assess contribution, evidence, structure, and format risks.
- Any score or recommendation must be based on the target venue's standards.

Check:
- Does the topic fit the venue?
- Does the abstract state the problem, method, materials, and contribution?
- Does the introduction establish research necessity?
- Does related work position the paper accurately?
- Is the method or analytical path credible?
- Do figures, cases, and data support the claims?
- Are references complete, accurate, and verifiable?
- Does the paper comply with format requirements?
- Are there risks around anonymity, ethics, copyright, or image permission?

Recommended structure:
Summary -> Strengths -> Critical Weaknesses -> Minor Issues -> Fit for Venue -> Revision Strategy.

Output:
- Part 1 [Review-style Evaluation]
- Part 2 [Critical Problems]
- Part 3 [Fixable Problems]
- Part 4 [Venue Fit Suggestions]
- Part 5 [Prioritized Revision Checklist]

## 7. Citation and Format Check

Use for GB/T 7714, APA, Chicago, ACM, and target-journal reference formatting and verification.

Rules:
- Do not fabricate citations.
- Mark unverifiable references as pending verification.
- Follow the target venue or journal format first.
- For Chinese journals, prioritize GB/T 7714 and journal-specific rules.
- For English conferences, prioritize ACM, APA, or Chicago according to venue requirements.

Check:
- Are authors, year, title, journal or conference name accurate?
- Are pages, volume, issue, and DOI complete where applicable?
- Do in-text citations and reference-list entries correspond?
- Are English translations of Chinese references appropriate?
- Do online sources include access dates if required?
- Do images, artworks, and exhibitions include source information?

Output:
- Part 1 [Format Issues]
- Part 2 [Suggested Revision]
- Part 3 [References Pending Verification]

## 8. Bilingual Translation and Alignment

Use for Chinese-to-English translation, English-to-Chinese translation, bilingual abstracts, titles, keywords, figure captions, and table captions.

Rules:
- Chinese-to-English translation should produce natural academic English rather than mechanical literal translation.
- English-to-Chinese translation should distinguish between reading-oriented literal translation and publication-ready Chinese.
- Key concepts must remain stable across Chinese and English.
- Titles, abstracts, and keywords must fit the target language and venue.
- Do not change academic meaning for fluency.

Check:
- Are terms consistent?
- Are theoretical concepts mistranslated?
- Are official names of journals and conferences preserved?
- Does the abstract fit the target language's academic style?
- Are keywords searchable?

Output:
- Part 1 [Translation]
- Part 2 [Terminology Table]
- Part 3 [Translation Notes]
